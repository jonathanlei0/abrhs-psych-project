The Hippocampus

The hippocampus is a seahorse-shaped structure in the body’s limbic system associated with emotions, desires, and memories; rather than dealing with psychodynamic theories, the hippocampus is the brain’s storage center for one’s conscious, explicit memories. However, how would external substances impact the function of the hippocampus? Drugs such as methamphetamine and cocaine have been heavily documented for their deleterious effects on the human body, but certain drugs — including cannabis or marjuana — have not been deemed as particularly harmful. With cannabis legalization becoming more widespread in the United States as a pain reliever, scientists have explored the structure of compounds inside marijuana that provide relief and potentially other undesirable effects on the hippocampus.[2]

Marijuana contains compounds known as 9-delta-tetrahydrocannabinols (THC) and that provide a pleasurable sensation.[3] THC is no new discovery; scientists extracted it from plants as early as the 1940s.[4] Regardless, because of increased marijuana usage amongst the general public, knowledge of the compound’s effects on the hippocampus is only progressing. THC is incredibly potent because our body possesses its own endocannabinoid system (ECS); the ECS comprises a vast network of chemical signals and cellular receptors that are densely packed throughout our brains and bodies. The cannabinoid-1 receptors (CB1) in the central nervous system (CNS) — meaning the brain and spinal cord — outnumber many of the other receptor types on the brain. They are the “traffic cops” that monitor the levels and activity of many other neurotransmitters. To stimulate these receptors, our bodies produce lipid neurotransmitters called endocannabinoids, which have a structural similarity to molecules in the cannabis plant. When endocannabinoids bond with CB1 receptors, the brain receives a signal to release dopamine, the “pleasure chemical.” The first endocannabinoid that was discovered was named anandamide after the Sanskrit word ananda for bliss. Simply put, we all possess tiny cannabis-like molecules swimming around in our brains.

THC as a Neurotransmitter

The cannabis plant, which humans have been using for about 5,000 years, hijacks this cellular machinery. Anandamide (C21H30O2) and tetrahydrocannabinol (C22H37O2) are lipids with very similar chemical structures; the two are very similar in molar mass and contain nonpolar regions (C-H) and polar regions (C-O) which can both interact with the CB1 receptor’s active site to trigger the release of dopamine. As such, THC serves as a competitive inhibitor, or a compound that can bind to the CB1 receptors and produce a pleasurable feeling via external intake of cannabis, “crowding out” natural endocannabinoids from binding to the CB1 receptors. What is more, THC signals the brain to release a significantly larger amount of dopamine than anandamide does, creating a significantly more pleasing experience for the cannabis user. As a result, the human brain signals for the body to produce fewer natural endocannabinoids for efficiency — the cannabis is providing them now and in greater quantities. However, once the THC effects wane, the human body now lacks an appropriate amount of endocannabinoids, occasionally leading to a cannabis withdrawal syndrome (CWS).

Effects of THC on the Hippocampus

Now that we know that THC can provide pleasure to our body at a higher level than anandamide, let’s ask this: how can THC affect other parts of the brain such as the hippocampus? Because CB1 receptors can be found all throughout the central nervous system, they are also present in the hippocampus. As such, the excessive dopamine released when THC bonds to these receptors can actually become more harmful than helpful; studies have shown that pre-teen, adolescent, and young adult marijuana usage are more likely to perform poorly in school and face conflict with the law. A current theory is that the overload of dopamine can lead to a loss of focus, ability to solve complex problems, and retain memories. The latter of the three is crucial when thinking about the hippocampus, our memory receptacle. In fact, more research has shown that chronic marijuana users have significantly smaller hippocampuses than people who do not use marijuana and exacerbate the age-related loss of hippocampal neurons, leading to cases of short term memory loss.[8][2] Certain conditions such as depression, which can make it difficult to pay attention as well, make an individual more prone to memory loss. Considering that nearly 25% of adults with depression use marijuana, damage to the hippocampus and consequent memory loss is easily exacerbated. Interestingly, chronic abstinence in marijuana users will return hippocampus size to normal, suggesting that marijuana’s effects, albeit harmful, may not be as permanent as opponents of the drug may claim.

Broader Implications

Thus, although THC’s addictive properties have empirically damaged the hippocampus and led to biological, psychological, and social alterations in cannabis users, these effects do not seem to be permanent. Cannabis’ widespread usage has only manifested in the last two decades, and states are only becoming more lenient with the drug; it remains to be seen what further effects THC has on hippocampus activity over a lifelong period.
